Kyoto in autumn, one temple at a time

By June Okada · October 14 · 12 min read

The city wakes slowly in October. By six, the mist still holds the eastern hills, and the first light catches the vermilion gates of Fushimi Inari long before the crowds arrive. If you climb only once, climb now — the mountain belongs to the foxes and to you.

The thousand gates of Fushimi Inari, an hour before opening.

Breakfast is best taken standing: a skewer of grilled mochi from the stall beside the shrine office, dusted with kinako. Then the long walk north along the Kamo river, where herons pose like garden statues and university crews slice the water into ribbons.

Nishiki Market rewards the patient. Skip the first row of stalls and go deeper — the knife shop that has sharpened steel for four hundred years, the pickle seller who will let you taste everything, the tiny counter where a grandmother sells exactly one kind of tamagoyaki, and only until noon.
